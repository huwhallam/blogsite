This project starts with hope and with frustration.

The frustration is familiar, I think. We live in a time of extraordinary technological change, where social media has transformed how we communicate, consume information, and form political opinions. It has also — and this is not a coincidence — produced an era of deepening political polarisation, rife with misinformation, division, and something that looks less like disagreement and more like mutual incomprehension.

I've spent years working with data and watching these dynamics play out, and I've come to believe that the crisis is partly — not entirely, but significantly — an **information architecture problem**. Current platforms are designed to maximise engagement, and engagement is maximised by outrage. They are algorithmically optimised to show people the most extreme, emotionally provocative versions of opposing views, rather than the most thoughtful ones. The result isn't just that people disagree: it's that they become very good at expressing their position loudly and very poor at engaging with the position opposite.

The architecture of how political information is organised, presented and engaged with is making us worse at politics.

## The Wikipedia moment

The spark for what I'm building came from hearing Jimmy Wales talk about Wikipedia. Here is an extraordinary resource — one I use every day — built on the idea that people with different knowledge and different perspectives can collaborate to produce something more accurate and more useful than any of them could alone. It works imperfectly, but it works.

What if that collaborative intent could be applied to contested political terrain? Not to adjudicate who is right, but to map the landscape: the competing positions, the evidence behind each, the values at stake, the strongest arguments on every side.

## What The Commons is

The Commons is a civic technology platform designed to do exactly that. Its organising principle is borrowed from Wikipedia but adapted for a different challenge: not facts, but disputes. Not knowledge that can be verified, but disagreement that needs to be understood.

The core idea is simple: for any significant political issue, build a structured, crowdsourced record of the full landscape of positions — each stated in its strongest form, each linked to its evidence, each situated among the interests and actors that hold it. Then make that record genuinely useful — to citizens trying to think through an issue, and to policymakers trying to understand what the public actually thinks and why.

AI runs through the platform, but not as an authority on what is true or right. Its role is Socratic: surfacing evidence you may not have seen, identifying logical fallacies in arguments, suggesting the strongest version of a view you're inclined to dismiss, asking what it would take for you to change your mind. The platform is designed to slow down rather than speed up. Friction, here, is a feature.

## What this blog is

I don't know how this project will turn out. I'm building it in public precisely because I think that transparency about the process — the decisions, the dead ends, the things I get wrong — is itself part of what I'm trying to model.

I also want your help. If this resonates with you — if you have relevant experience, ideas, objections, or just enthusiasm — please [get in touch](/contact). The Commons will only work if people want to build it together.
