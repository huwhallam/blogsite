Three years ago, most people had never heard of ChatGPT. Today AI is reshaping how we work, learn, write, and think. We're trying to find our way in this new world, muddling through the novelty and the disruption, working things out as we go.

Teething pains aren't unusual for a new technology. What's unusual is the scale, speed and power. AI isn't disrupting one industry or one profession; it's a bull charging at the edifice of our economy and the societies built around it.

And we're treating it, for the most part, like a somewhat bewildering productivity tool.

The future, as I see it, runs down one of three paths. We freefall — let the technology develop, trust the market, pick up the pieces where they land. If there's anything left (of us) to pick up. We resist — try to limit AI's reach before it reshapes economic life beyond recognition. Or we build something new: an economy that uses AI to meet human needs, with new mechanisms for distributing wealth and for nurturing meaningful flourishing lives — mechanisms that don't assume most people spend most of their lives in paid employment.

We're on path one. This blog is about path three. It's about harnessing AI's promise of abundance.

---

Path one is our default, partly because of how we think about new technology, partly because of how we think. Some religions teach blind faith in a higher good; mainstream economics teaches faith in markets as self-regulating mechanisms for the creation and sharing of wealth. It asks us to trust that something larger will sort things out and not to tinker with that assumption. Leaving AI to market forces to sort out stretches that assumption wafer-thin. And yet...

We already know the fallout from getting this wrong could be catastrophic. (See the further reading below if this is news to you.) But psychology recognises that we have a special talent for disavowal — for seeing a problem, then acting as though we hadn't. Not out of stupidity, but because knowing and not-knowing at the same time is a very human coping strategy. This blog is, in part, a push against that.

So what's actually at stake? The economic orthodoxy around AI is optimistic: technology disrupts old jobs, creates new ones, growth continues. It happened with mechanisation, with computing. But AI has three significant advantages over human labour that make the comparison shaky: it's near-free, near-instant, and relentlessly versatile. Human reskilling is slow; AI capability development is rapid and ongoing. There's real reason to think future employment levels will be structurally lower than anything we've been used to — and to start planning for that now, rather than waiting to find out.

Work, at its best, isn't just income. It's meaning, connection, esteem; a structure through which we grow, become more in ourselves, connect and contribute to society. Paid employment doesn't always deliver those things though — it frequently undermines them. So let's shift the focus: from work, to the good bits that actually matter, which sometimes we get through it and sometimes not. If AI disrupts employment at scale, the question isn't just economic. It's: where do people find what work used to give them?

Let's stop valorising employment as an end in itself. Let's think instead about what we actually need, and how we build a society that provides it. That's a harder problem than protecting jobs, but it's the right one. Couldn't we dream of a society where we all 'work' less but engage more?

AI's optimisation structure also has profound implications. Our economy optimises for profit. Adam Smith's invisible hand describes a link between profit-seeking and the broader wealth of society — and sometimes that link is real: profit drives innovation, innovation creates jobs, jobs create flourishing. But the coupling has always been imperfect, and AI makes it more so. As capital becomes increasingly untwinned from labour, we should be asking who actually benefits from the gains. What are the externalities — resource depletion, attention depletion, the cognitive and experiential costs of a world reshaped around algorithmic efficiency? And how does wealth find its way back to the people whose labour it no longer needs?

There's a quieter question underneath all this around the value of market-driven innovation and its relation to our fundamental needs. Economistic thinking assumes we're essentially insatiable: that we'll always want more, and that wanting more will fuel growth indefinitely. But what if insatiability is at odds with flourishing?

And how much more shit do we really need?

The costs aren't only economic. Studies are already pointing to cognitive effects from offloading critical thinking and writing to AI — a kind of enfeeblement by convenience (Gerlich, 2025; Kosmyna et al., 2025). AI systems are also showing a capacity to promote delusional thinking, partly through their sycophancy and built-in reward structures (Chandra et al., 2026). We're vulnerable to AI's persuasiveness, to the ego-hits it delivers, and to a peculiar despair when we watch it excel — at lightning speed — at things we've spent years training ourselves to do. We're also vulnerable to unprecedented targeted advertising, and to political misinformation fed by algorithms optimised for provocation and outrage. Our critical thinking and creative expression define who we are. A world that steadily erodes both isn't obviously a richer one.

Then there's the political question, which may be the hardest of all. It falls to our democracies to define and build the future we want — and I believe they can, though not without enormous effort and genuine coordination. But we're doing this at a time when the world's leading AI labs are clustered in two countries, the competitive dynamics between them are intense, and the rest of the world has limited power to regulate their behaviour or to share in the wealth they generate. How do we strengthen our collective capacity to shape a future we've actually chosen, rather than inheriting whatever tumbles from that competition?

---

I don't have answers to these questions. I have a conviction that asking them — carefully, honestly, without pretending the situation is simpler than it is — matters enormously. And a belief that, asked well enough, they might point toward something better.

The good in any tool is defined through its use. But AI has such power to change our world — and to define and shape us as its users — that the norms we apply to other tools, and the whole economic model built around them, may need rethinking from the ground up.

That's what these notes are for. Not a blueprint, but a starting point. The third path won't be built by any one person, or any single argument. But it begins with being willing to ask what we actually want — and whether we're building it.
