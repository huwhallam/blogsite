There's a version of the future where advanced AI makes everyone vastly better off. There's another version where it concentrates power and resources in fewer and fewer hands, or where misaligned systems cause harm at scale.

The difference between those futures isn't just technical. It's also about choices — political, economic, institutional — that we're making now, mostly without enough deliberation.

This blog is about that gap.

## Why connect AI safety to economics?

The usual framing of AI safety is technical: alignment problems, reward hacking, interpretability, robustness under distribution shift. These are real and important. But they're not the whole story.

Economic structures shape incentives. Incentives shape what gets built and how. A lab under pressure to ship products may deprioritise safety in ways that a differently-structured institution wouldn't. A world where AI systems are held by a handful of entities creates different risks than one where they're more broadly accessible and governed.

You can't fully separate "will AI systems behave as intended?" from "whose intentions are they serving?" and "what institutions are holding them accountable?"

## What I'm trying to figure out

- How do alignment and governance interact? Does making systems more technically robust also make them easier to govern, or does it sometimes make them harder?
- What economic models make safety-first development viable?
- What does "abundance" actually mean, and is it the right frame?

I don't have settled answers to these questions. I have hunches and reading and a growing sense that they matter. That's what I'll be working through here.
